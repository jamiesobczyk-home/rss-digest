Lessons learned from scaling to 1 million Lambda functions

ProGlove scaled a serverless SaaS platform to over 1 million AWS Lambda functions across thousands of accounts using an account-per-tenant architecture, discovering that true scale-to-zero is critical and that traditional practices like SQS polling become cost drivers at massive scale. Key lessons included eliminating synchronized schedules via jitter to prevent "self-DDoS" attacks, centralizing observability to control costs, replacing queues with metric-driven safety patterns, and engaging AWS service teams early to optimize deployment performance. Success required automation, mono-repo discipline, and efficiency improvements that scale faster than customer growth.

Dual-token authentication for Nakama game servers with Amazon Cognito on AWS

Nakama game servers can authenticate players through dual tokens: Amazon Cognito issues JWTs for player identity, while a Go runtime hook validates these JWTs and exchanges them for Nakama session tokens, letting each system manage its own lifecycle independently. The infrastructure layers requests through CloudFront, AWS WAF, and an ALB with an explicit route allow-list for HTTP API traffic, while WebSocket connections bypass the ALB directly to an NLB for TCP passthrough to Nakama on ECS. WebSocket lifecycle management uses four controls—NLB TCP idle timeout (350s), Nakama's 10-second ping interval, 20-second pong wait, and token expiry at connect time—to prevent connection stalls while blocking stale sessions.

Is It Safe to Store Leftovers in Your Hotel Fridge? The Science Behind It Might Surprise You

Many hotel mini fridges are actually beverage coolers that only reach 40-45°F or higher, rather than the 40°F needed to safely store food, putting leftovers in the bacterial "danger zone" where pathogens multiply rapidly. Perishable foods shouldn't stay above 40°F for more than two hours or they become unsafe to eat. Solutions include bringing a refrigerator thermometer to verify temperature, requesting a real refrigerator before arrival, or storing only shelf-stable foods like crackers and jerky.

The US Supreme Court restricts use of geofence warrants

The US Supreme Court ruled 6-3 that geofence warrants violate the Fourth Amendment, establishing that individuals have a reasonable expectation of privacy in cell phone location data. Law enforcement agencies must now obtain traditional search warrants with probable cause to access location information from tech companies, rather than using geofence warrants that previously allowed bulk searches of users near crime scenes. The decision arose from a Virginia bank robbery case where detectives used Google location data to identify suspects without suspicion of the millions of users whose information was searched.
