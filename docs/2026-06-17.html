U.S. Science Is in Chaos

NASA's billion-dollar AXIS space telescope project, led by astronomer Christopher Reynolds, was canceled after years of development due to cascading budget pressures and administrative chaos. The mission lost critical personnel when NASA offered buyouts in response to cuts proposed by the Department of Government Efficiency, and the subsequent federal government shutdown and cost overruns left the team unable to meet revised timelines and budget requirements. The collapse of AXIS exemplifies how broken funding structures and political interference are undermining U.S. scientific research, since about 40 percent of basic research funding comes from the federal government.

Hacker News · 4:28 AM

Show HN: High-Res Neural Cellular Automata

Neural Cellular Automata (NCAs)—bio-inspired systems where cells self-organize via learned local rules—have been limited to low-resolution outputs due to computational and memory constraints. Researchers overcome this by pairing a coarse-grid NCA with a lightweight implicit decoder that maps cell states to appearance at arbitrary resolution, enabling real-time high-resolution rendering. The approach preserves NCAs' characteristic self-organizing behavior while maintaining parallelizable inference.

Slashdot · 2:00 AM

AI and Brain-Computer Interface Allow Speechless ALS Patient To Work a Full-Time Job

UC Davis researchers have demonstrated that a brain-computer interface implanted in ALS patient Casey Harrell enables him to synthesize sentences from brain activity with 99% accuracy in controlled tests and 92% in everyday use. Unlike previous BCI systems requiring researcher supervision, Harrell's implant has operated independently from his home since 2023, allowing him to work full-time, control a computer, and communicate naturally with family and colleagues. He has logged over 3,800 hours of use on the system, averaging more than five hours daily.

Hacker News · 1:50 AM

From Chesterton's fence to Chesterton's gap

Chesterton's fence is a principle urging programmers to understand why code was written a certain way before changing it, fostering empathy and acknowledging their own knowledge limits. The author introduces "Chesterton's gap" as the inverse problem: developers now submit large, unsolicited pull requests adding features, tools, and configurations that weren't requested, failing to ask why a project maintainer didn't add them in the first place.
