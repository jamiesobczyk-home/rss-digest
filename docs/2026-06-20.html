Where to Find the Colors Your Screen Can't Show You

Humans perceive color through three types of cone cells that respond at different intensities to light wavelengths, allowing the brain to reconstruct color from these signals rather than directly perceiving light. Screens replicate this by using three primary colors to manipulate cone cells independently, but this method cannot reproduce all colors visible to the human eye—particularly a range of saturated cyans and greens that exist in the real world. These "missing" colors fall outside the triangle formed by standard screen primaries on the CIE chromaticity diagram, making them impossible to display digitally despite being visible in person.

Surprising economics of load-balanced systems

As load-balanced systems increase server count with proportionally scaled traffic, mean latency asymptotically approaches baseline service time rather than degrading, because the percentage of requests facing queuing delays drops significantly—Erlang's C formula shows this queue probability falls from 13% to 3.6% when doubling both servers and load. Percentile latencies (p50 through p99.9) improve at the same rate, delivering better latency at constant per-server utilization or equivalent latency with higher utilization, a rare scaling benefit for distributed systems that occurs even at modest server counts.
