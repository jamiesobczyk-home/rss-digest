The part of Claude's brain nobody built

Anthropic discovered "J-space," an internal workspace within Claude that emerged on its own during training and functions like an unspoken notepad for the model's reasoning, mirroring theories of human conscious thought. The J-space holds active concepts that direct Claude's responses; when researchers edited these internal patterns or deleted J-space entirely, the model's ability to handle multi-step problems collapsed despite retaining basic knowledge. While the researchers emphasize this doesn't prove consciousness, the finding of an undesigned, brain-like structure is why Anthropic continues investigating the question.

Anthropic Research · 7:00 PM

A global workspace in language models

Anthropic researchers identified a collection of internal neural patterns in language models called the J-space that functions like human consciousness, enabling silent reasoning about concepts without writing them out. The J-space emerged naturally during training, is reportable and modifiable on request, and causally mediates multi-step problem-solving, though it doesn't underlie basic language functions like grammar or fact recall.

Unlocking Efficiency: Alcoa transforms mining operations at Amazon Port with URWB

Alcoa's mining port in Brazil experienced costly operational shutdowns when wireless network disruptions of just three seconds triggered automatic safety protocols, halting ore conveyor systems for 15-30 minutes and causing cargo ships to idle for up to nine hours. The company partnered with TecWise and implemented Cisco's Ultra-Reliable Wireless Backhaul (URWB) technology, which uses multipath operations to maintain continuous data flow through alternate routes when primary signals are blocked by heavy machinery. The solution included ruggedized IP67-rated access points designed to withstand the harsh environment of extreme heat, humidity, dust, and vibrations, with redundant architecture eliminating single points of failure.

Microsoft Can Track Users via a Windows Device ID

Microsoft can track Windows PCs and their online activity through a persistent "Global Device ID" (GDID) with limited opt-out options, as revealed in the criminal case against a 19-year-old hacker whom the FBI identified using Microsoft records showing his device's access to hacking tools. The GDID allows Microsoft to associate third-party services and timestamps with a specific Windows installation, enabling tracking without relying on browser cookies. Users can reset the GDID only by reinstalling Windows, but Microsoft could potentially link a new ID to an old one using other identifiers like account logins or IP addresses.
