Under federal rule, colleges must leave grads better off or lose financial aid

Unexpected Solidlike Fracture in Simple Liquids

Researchers at Drexel University discovered that simple, non-elastic viscous fluids can fracture like brittle solids when subjected to stress—a phenomenon previously thought to require the elasticity found in complex fluids. While studying a hydrogen-carbon blend, Professor Thamires Lima observed the fluid breaking apart with an audible crack when pulled at high speeds, and subsequent testing confirmed this brittle fracture occurs repeatedly, not as a one-time anomaly. This finding challenges the long-held assumption that elasticity is a prerequisite for solid-like fracturing behavior in fluids.

The Energetic Costs of Cellular Computation (2012)

Mehta and Schwab calculate the energetic costs of cellular computation, showing that cells must consume energy to learn chemical ligand concentrations, with greater accuracy demanding more energy—consistent with Landauer's Principle. Their analysis of a simple two-component network model reveals that environmental sensing requires breaking detailed balance in cellular systems. The energetic constraints identified may fundamentally limit information processing in resource-poor environments such as bacterial spore germination networks.
