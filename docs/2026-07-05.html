Web-based cryptography is always snake oil

Web-based "end-to-end" encryption services are fundamentally fraudulent because the server operator distributes the client-side code, allowing them to unilaterally modify it—making any security claims incoherent. This same flaw affects services like WhatsApp and Signal that control their own client distribution and ban third-party alternatives. Companies adopt this "snake oil cryptography" not for genuine security but as legal theater, enabling them to claim inability to comply with government warrants and court orders.

Fast Software, the Best Software

Craig Mod argues that software speed is the most valuable yet least-valued asset, as fast software integrates smoothly into users' lives while slow software feels burdensome. Speed often signals overall engineering quality and reliability; when software slows on simple tasks, it undermines user trust and suggests deeper problems. Mod illustrates this through examples like nvALT (nearly instant text searching), Ulysses (occasionally slow on larger documents despite good features), and Sublime Text (extremely fast but less refined for writing).

Hacker News · 12:56 AM

Megawatts by Microwave

The article traces how the Army Corps of Engineers and the Bureau of Reclamation competed to develop Columbia River hydroelectric dams, starting with early 20th-century investigations and intensifying during President Franklin D. Roosevelt's New Deal, which favored hydropower as an economic stimulus during the Great Depression. FDR's Regional Planning Commission outlined a series of Columbia dams, including the Grand Coulee and Bonneville, designed to provide flood control, irrigation, and power generation, with Bonneville Dam completed in 1938 as the first major project. The Bonneville Dam prioritized electrical generation over water storage, producing up to 1.2 GW—a significant capacity for the 1930s era.
